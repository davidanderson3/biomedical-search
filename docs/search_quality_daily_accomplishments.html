June 16, 2026 Accomplishments

Added 2026-06-17. This translates the June 16 iteration run into the practical story: patient wording became clean enough to trust in the measured lane, and the next wrong-first risks were made visible.

Made Patient Wording Recall Complete

Every reviewed short patient phrase in the consumer wording lane now finds the intended medical concept in the first 10 answers. This includes everyday phrases such as blood thinner, belly pain, pee test, EKG, and scope wording.

Removed First-Page Literal Drift

The same patient-wording lane no longer shows the configured literal or anatomy/device drift in the first page. Water-pill wording no longer brings in water or hydrotherapy noise, sugar-diabetes wording no longer brings in generic sugar concepts, and scope wording no longer surfaces unrelated anatomy/device answers.

Improved Public Result Hygiene

Internal definition-support signals are now stripped from public result sources instead of leaking implementation details into returned hits. Active-label hits that are supported by reviewed patient wording can stay visible as normal public answers.

Also Left Clear Gaps

June 16 did not finish search quality. The consumer lane still needs a gate-policy decision, the latest rotating smoke found two fresh wrong-first rows outside the reviewed product lanes, and realistic note-format recall remains the weakest measured workflow.

June 15, 2026 Accomplishments

Added 2026-06-16. This translates the June 15 iteration run into the practical story: the work made coverage risk visible, improved patient wording support, closed the core source-specific gate, added a molecular exploration opt-in, and fixed one held-out PubMed recall miss.

Measured What The Tests Actually Cover

The suite now reports which medical concepts it is testing instead of treating a green run as proof of broad clinical coverage. The audit found that the test set is still heavily weighted toward disorders, with thin anatomy, physiology, and phenomena coverage.

Added Governed Patient-Wording Aliases

Common phrases such as water pill, pee test, belly pain, sugar diabetes, heart tracing, EKG, colon scope, stomach scope, and blood thinner now have reviewed alias support. The live consumer lane still needs a fresh rerun before the headline score can move.

Closed The Core Source-Specific Gate

The DailyMed warning-context miss was fixed without globally promoting the generic word warning. The core source-specific lane for DailyMed, MedlinePlus, and PubMed/PMC reached 9/9 rows with all expected concepts in the first 10 answers.

Made Gene And Protein Exploration Explicit

The search UI now has a default-off Explore genes/proteins control. Normal clinical searches keep relation-derived molecular associations out of the main result list unless the user asks for that exploration mode.

Fixed One Held-Out PubMed Recall Miss

The fidaxomicin-versus-vancomycin abstract now surfaces the C. difficile infection concept in the first 10 answers on the targeted live rerun. The full approved PubMed suite still needs a fresh rerun before the broader PubMed card can improve.

Also Left Clear Gaps

June 15 did not finish search quality. Consumer wording still needs a live rerun, the approved PubMed suite still has long-document recall misses, and representative concept coverage still needs reviewed quotas rather than only an audit.

June 12, 2026 Accomplishments

Added 2026-06-15. This translates the June 12 iteration run into the practical story: the work rotated across several high-priority risks, made weak spots measurable, narrowed the source strategy, and closed the last reviewed PubMed wrong-first issue.

Stopped The Loop From Getting Stuck

The backlog now rotates across several top tasks instead of repeatedly selecting one open item. A proposed short-query expansion was tested live and rejected because it exposed real exact-mention carryover failures rather than passing safely.

Made Patient Wording Failures Visible

Short consumer wording such as water pill, pee test, belly pain, heart tracing, scopes, and blood thinner now has its own measured test lane. The first run failed, which is useful because the product can no longer hide that everyday wording is not handled well enough.

Narrowed The Source Strategy

Default source checks were pruned back to DailyMed, MedlinePlus, and PubMed/PMC. ClinicalTrials.gov, PubTator3, CUI2Vec, and BioConceptVec remain experiment or opt-in signals unless they prove a concrete gap. DailyMed stayed core because its label sections carry useful drug relationship evidence.

Kept Molecular Associations Out Of Default Results

Gene and protein associations inferred only from relationship data are now hidden from normal search results unless molecular exploration is explicitly requested. Direct EGFR, BRCA1, CYP2C19, mutation, and variant searches can still work.

Closed The Last Reviewed PubMed Wrong-First Row

The status-migrainosus abstract no longer ranks a broad corticosteroid class ahead of the directly relevant treatment concepts. The approved PubMed suite is now top-on-target for all reviewed rows, though several secondary concepts still need recall work.

Found The Long-Document Speed Bottleneck

The slow focused PubMed loop was instrumented inside the server. The result showed that long-document mention extraction, not vector search, is the dominant local runtime cost.

Also Left Clear Gaps

June 12 did not finish search quality. Consumer lay wording is still weak, the source-specific lane still misses one DailyMed warning-context concept, the approved PubMed suite still has recall misses, and long-document searches remain too slow for comfortable iteration.

June 11, 2026 Accomplishments

Added 2026-06-12. This translates the June 11 iteration run into the practical story: search became better at keeping the main clinical idea first, the long-article tests became more current, and the next work became easier to rotate across.

Kept The Main Problem First

Several wrong-first failures were fixed. Diagnoses such as gestational diabetes, cystic fibrosis, lead poisoning, anaphylaxis, pulmonary embolism, and opioid-use-disorder treatment context now beat nearby symptom, test, trigger, or broad behavior noise when the wording supports that order.

Improved Patient Message Search

Patient-portal messages now keep the current issue first across the reviewed lane. Generic background wording is less likely to outrank the actual problem the patient is asking about.

Made PubMed Article Search More Useful

The focused PubMed abstract probe reached a clean first-page recall result. The broader approved PubMed suite was rerun and reclassified, replacing a stale baseline with a current view of exactly which article-search misses remain.

Promoted Short And Source-Specific Checks

Reviewed short real-world searches became a strict release lane, and source-specific checks exposed where DailyMed, PubMed/PMC, clinical-trial, MedlinePlus, and PubTator3 behavior still needs attention.

Reduced Bad Broad Matches

Broad matches such as generic cancer screening, broad addictive behavior, and overbroad drug classes were made less likely to appear above directly named clinical targets.

Made Iteration Time Visible

The benchmark runner now records per-query timing and can overlap live API calls. That did not solve the slow long-document path by itself, but it showed that the remaining bottleneck is inside the search API rather than the report writer.

Made The Backlog Easier To Work

The progress log now uses stable backlog IDs with a current gap, next diff, artifact, and done condition, so each iteration can be picked up without rereading every prior artifact.

Also Left Clear Gaps

June 11 did not finish the product. The source-specific lane still has a DailyMed warning-context miss, the approved PubMed suite still has article-search misses, and the long-document path is still too slow for comfortable repeated iteration.

June 10, 2026 Accomplishments

Added 2026-06-11. This translates the June 10 work into the practical story: what got safer, what got easier to trust, and what new weak spots became visible.

Made Every Change Safer

We added a repeatable smoke-check workflow so search changes now have to prove they still work after the fix. Instead of trusting one hand-picked example, the loop now records a reusable pass/fail report.

Added More Real-Life Tests

The test set grew beyond neat clinical paragraphs. It now includes long patient messages, simple everyday searches, source-specific searches, and long medical article checks.

Improved Long Article Search

PubMed-style abstracts became a first-class test lane. The focused article probe improved from finding too few expected ideas to finding many more of them on the first page, while keeping known bad matches out.

Separated Useful Extras From Bad Answers

We reviewed the suspect-result pile and sorted it into useful extra results versus true bad matches. That made the next cleanup target much clearer instead of treating every extra result as wrong.

Fixed Visible Search Problems

Several concrete failures were fixed or guarded: a UMLS-only crash, missing results for clear terms like gastroenterology and appendectomy, a diagnosis that was ranked below a later culture organism, and a common word being mistaken for a gene.

Made Source-Code Search More Trustworthy

Public source-code searches can now return allowed RxNorm and LOINC rows instead of dropping everything or mixing in unrelated concept-style results.

Made The Work Easier To Audit

The reports were reorganized so each iteration says what weakness was found, what was tried, what passed, what failed, and what should happen next.

Also Made The Remaining Gaps Clearer

June 10 did not make search perfect. It showed that long articles, wrong-first results, source-document retrieval, and overly noisy answers still needed more work.
